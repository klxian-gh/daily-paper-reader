Abstract 

The high-precision solution of the radial Dirac equation is fundamental to relativistic quantum chem-istry, essential for reliable pseudopotential generation and all-electron electronic structure methods. How-ever, standard basis-set approaches struggle to simultaneously capture two distinct physical regimes: the non-polynomial singularities at the origin and the state-dependent, multi-scale asymptotic decay of wave-functions on semi-infinite domains. In this work, we propose a high-precision adaptive spectral-element framework designed to rigorously resolve these spatial challenges. To capture the diverse exponential de-cay behavior on [0 , ∞) without arbitrary domain truncation, an adaptive scaled Laguerre spectral method is introduced, dynamically optimizing the basis scaling factors. Concurrently, near-origin non-polynomial 

rs singularities are resolved utilizing Log-Orthogonal Functions, a basis that intrinsically approximates complex singular behaviors without requiring prior knowledge of the exact analytical exponent s. Fur-thermore, the framework incorporates an inverse operator formulation to guarantee spectral purity and eliminate spurious states. Validated across diverse physical regimes, including Coulomb, finite-nucleus, and screened potentials, the proposed method restores exponential convergence and consistently achieves relative accuracies of 10 −10 in Hartree atomic units or electron volts. This work provides a robust, non-pollution computational kernel for atomic structure calculations, establishing a numerical standard for generating high-precision atomic data in complex molecular simulations.

# 1 Introduction 

The Radial Dirac Equation (RDEQ) is the heart of high-fidelity atomic structure calculations, which are the indispensable computational kernel for modern relativistic electronic structure methods [1], including Density Functional Theory [2] and ab initio pseudopotential generation [3, 4]. Traditionally, the RDEQ is tackled using either shooting methods [4–6] or matrix diagonalization via basis set expansions, utilizing B-splines or finite elements [7–10]. However, achieving the high precision demanded by quantum chemistry is severely limited by the mathematical behavior of the radial wavefunctions, since traditional numerical schemes are primarily hindered by two major spatial bottlenecks: the state-dependent asymptotic decay on the far-field domain and non-polynomial singularities near the origin. Moreover, basis set approaches must handle the intrinsic and potential risk of variational collapse [11–14]. Failing to rigorously resolve these challenges, while maintaining spectral stability, compromises the reliability of large-scale molecular simulations. Fundamentally, resolving the two bottlenecks is a challenge of function approximation, which is the pri-mary focus of this paper. At the far-field boundary, a significant bottleneck arises because bound-state wavefunctions exhibit exponential decay over the semi-infinite physical domain [0 , ∞). Because their precise decay rates and effective supports are state-dependent and unknown a priori, they are difficult to approxi-mate. Despite its critical impact on overall precision, this specific challenge has frequently been overlooked or marginalized in existing literature. Rather than addressing the decay behavior, conventional methods often circumvent the issue by enforcing an empirical domain truncation at a finite distance L, relying on a trial-and-error approach that struggles to balance high fidelity with computational efficiency [15, 16]. Conversely, near the origin, an equally tough challenge originates from potentials that diverge at the origin. Specifically, for models exhibiting a infinity singularity, such as the classic Coulomb potential or screened Yukawa poten-tials where lim r→0 V (r) ∼ − Z/r asymptotic analysis dictates that the wavefunctions behave as rs. Because 

s is non-integer, this induces a severe non-polynomial singularity. Consequently, classical polynomial-based methods, such as high-order finite element methods, fail to capture this fractional-power behavior, leading to drastically deteriorated convergence rates. Attempts to analytically extract the rs singularity complicate the variational formulation [10], while finite-element geometric mesh refinements [17] significantly increase implementation complexity. Although existing specialized basis sets are explicitly designed for isolated singularities, their potential remains largely unexplored in relativistic atomic physics. Furthermore, any numerical scheme designed to resolve these dual spatial challenges must simultaneously guarantee spectral stability against the ever-present threat of variational collapse, necessitating robust strategies to prevent spectral pollution. To systematically overcome these interconnected bottlenecks, we propose a comprehensive, high-order adaptive spectral framework tailored for relativistic atomic calculations. Because a structurally stable com-putational kernel is a basic requirement for achieving extreme precision, our framework natively incorporates the Inverse Dirac Operator Method (IDOM) [18]. This algebraic formulation rigorously circumvents varia-tional collapse, guaranteeing the absolute spectral purity of the computed bound states. Built upon this vari-ation stable foundation, we introduce two combined innovations to directly resolve both the non-polynomial singularities and the multi-scale asymptotic decay. 

• Adaptive Laguerre method : To capture the state-dependent asymptotic decay on the semi-infinite domain without resorting to empirical truncations, we introduce an adaptive Laguerre method [19]. By treating the frequency indicator of the basis functions as an optimization parameter, this approach automatically aligns the basis scaling with the physical wavefunction’s far-field tail. To the best of our knowledge, this represents one of the first systematic applications of adaptive spectral methods specifically optimized for Dirac eigenvalue problems. This innovation overcomes the inefficiency of fixed-scale bases, proving that standard orthogonal families, such as Laguerre functions (LFs), can be rigorously and efficiently applied to quantum mechanical calculations when properly scaled. 

• Log-Orthogonal Functions : To resolve non-polynomial singularities at the origin, we use LOFs [20], which ensures an efficient treatment of the singularity. Crucially, the LOF basis provides a “black-box” capability to intrinsically approximate complex singular behaviors, such as the fractional rs dependence, without requiring any prior analytical specification of the exponent s. This avoids the need for complex analytical extractions or dense geometric mesh refinements, opening the door for applications to more complex relativistic formulations where the exact nature of the singularity may be intractable [21]. 2Validated across a spectrum of challenging physical regimes, including Coulomb, finite-nucleus, and screened interactions, this unified framework consistently achieves relative accuracies of 10 −10 or better, successfully restoring exponential convergence where standard bases typically deteriorate. A detailed in-vestigation is conducted into the numerical properties, specifically examining how truncation limits affect the resolution of spectral and how degree of freedom imbalances give rise to spurious states. Meanwhile, we demonstrate that reformulating the Dirac operator formulation is essential for eliminating spectral pollution. Ultimately, this work establishes a highly stable, pollution-free computational engine for relativistic atomic calculations, providing the rigorous numerical standard necessary to fortify modern large-scale electronic structure methodologies. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 establishes the theoretical foundation of our approach, introducing the continuous RDEQ alongside a rigorous analysis of the exact asymptotic behavior of the wavefunctions. It also presents methodologies to address these approximation challenges, notably through the implementation of Generalized Log-Orthogonal Functions (GLOFs) and adaptive Laguerre methods. Section 3 outlines the practical computational implementation, detailing the construction of the globally continuous spectral basis and deriving the variationally robust Galerkin formulation via IDOM. In Section 4, we comprehensively evaluate the proposed framework. This section establishes high-precision numerical benchmarks across a diverse array of chemically and physically relevant potentials. Meanwhile, we provide a profound theoretical dissection of spectral stability, elucidating the topological origins of variational collapse and the critical role of degree of freedom mismatch in Section 5. Finally, Section 6 summarizes our core methodological contributions and outlines the trajectory for future applications in large-scale relativistic electronic structure calculations.

# 2.3 Adaptive Laguerre Method for Asymptotic Decay 

For quantum mechanical problems defined on a semi-unbounded domain [0 , ∞), the Laguerre basis functions emerge as the most natural and mathematically rigorous choice. Unlike finite-element or bounded polynomial methods that necessitate an artificial domain truncation, a heuristic process that inevitably introduces unphysical boundary artifacts and truncates essential far-field information, Laguerre functions intrinsically possess an exponential tail. This guarantees that the physical asymptotic behavior is structurally preserved without requiring empirical spatial cutoffs. However, Laguerre basis fixed characteristic length scale is fundamentally ill-suited for the multi-scale, state-dependent decay of relativistic wavefunctions. As illustrated in Figures 1 and 2, capturing highly oscillatory near-field nodes alongside diffuse far-field tails with a fixed-scale basis requires a prohibitive number of degrees of freedom. To restore exponential convergence without artificially inflating the basis size, we introduce a dynamically optimized scaling parameter β. This yields the GLFs cL βn (r), which effectively 6“stretches” or “compresses” the coordinate system to perfectly envelop the physical support of the target eigenstate. The central computational challenge thus shifts from simply increasing N to efficiently identifying the optimal β without trial-and-error. Inspired by adaptive spectral methods [19], we treat β as a dynamic optimization parameter governed by a frequency indicator , F. This indicator quantifies the relative energy content residing in the high-frequency tail of the computed eigenfunction U βN (r) = PNl=0 cβl cL βl (r). When employing the truncated Legendre basis, the approximation essentially confines the quantum sys-tem within an artificial finite box of radius L. This comparative analysis yields a critical numerical insight: for relativistic atomic wavefunctions char-acterized by multi-scale, state-dependent asymptotic tails, finite-interval domain truncation strategies are fundamentally structurally limited. Semi-unbounded bases, such as the Laguerre family, are not merely a convenient choice, but a strict mathematical requisite for capturing the complete discrete spectrum without inducing artificial physical distortion.

# 5.2 Topological Origins of Variational Collapse and DOF Mismatch 

Variational collapse remains one of the most notorious pathologies in relativistic quantum chemistry since its early diagnosis by Schwarz and Wallmeier [11, 33]. For decades, the physical chemistry community has predominantly attributed the emergence of these spurious “prolapse” states to a rather intuitive physical cause: the fact that the Dirac Hamiltonian ˆD is not bounded from below, which ostensibly causes standard variational procedures to “fall” into the negative-energy Dirac sea[11–13]. Consequently, conventional reme-dies, such as the widely adopted kinetic balance condition, were developed primarily to enforce the correct non-relativistic Schrödinger limit and artificially stave off this collapse. However, this pragmatic physical intuition fundamentally obscures the rigorous mathematical root of the instability. The true mechanism of variational collapse is not merely a consequence of lacking a lower bound; rather, its origins are strictly topological [14]. Because the essential spectrum of ˆD is fundamentally non-connected, 17 (−∞ , −c2] ∪ [c2, ∞), the physical bound-state mass gap (−c2, c 2) is left completely topologically exposed. In standard finite basis representations, it is precisely this structural vulnerability that allows spurious eigenvalues to freely invade the gap and severely contaminate the physical spectrum. This topological perspective elegantly explains why purely confining potentials naturally suppress spectral pollution: they push the essential spectrum to infinity, yielding a purely discrete spectrum devoid of a central gap. In contrast, realistic molecular potentials that vanish at infinity inevitably resurrect the non-connected Dirac sea, dictating that numerical stability must rely on a mathematically rigorous discretization scheme rather than empirical physical constraints. A critical source of this spectral contamination is the imbalance in the degrees of freedom between the upper spinor components F with N basis and the lower spinor components G with K basis. Our numerical investigations reveal a highly directional pollution mechanism governed by this mismatch. When the upper DOF exceeds the lower, N > K , spurious solutions predominantly descend from the positive continuum, clustering densely within the upper region of the mass gap and directly obscuring the physically relevant bound states, as evidenced by the highlighted values in Table 4. Conversely, an excess of lower-component DOF, K > N , forcefully drives spurious states upward toward the positive continuum. Consequently, when strictly targeting low-lying bound states, a K > N configuration is structurally permissible, as the pollution is repelled away from the physical ground state. Furthermore, in the perfectly balanced N = K

configuration, no spurious states are observed across our tested potentials. This is a direct testament to the extreme efficiency of ALLSM, in which the required basis size is simply too compact to provide the topological flexibility necessary for variational collapse to manifest.
